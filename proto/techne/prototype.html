Photolithography

The precision patterning technique that enables the fabrication of integrated circuits by transferring geometric patterns onto semiconductor substrates through optical exposure and chemical development.

02

Origin

Historical Genesis

1826

Photographic Origins

Nicéphore Niépce creates first permanent photograph using bitumen of Judea, establishing photochemical patterning principle.

1955

First Semiconductor Application

Jay Lathrop at Diamond Ordnance Fuze Laboratory uses photolithography for transistor fabrication, solving the precision patterning problem for microelectronics.

1959

Planar Process Integration

Jean Hoerni at Fairchild Semiconductor invents the planar process, making photolithography foundational to integrated circuit manufacturing.

1980s-Present

Wavelength Reduction Era

Continuous progression: G-line (436nm) → I-line (365nm) → KrF (248nm) → ArF (193nm) → EUV (13.5nm), each enabling smaller feature sizes.

Constraint Origin

The technique emerged from the need to create precisely defined microscopic patterns on semiconductor substrates. Early approaches:

  • × Hand-drawn masks (inconsistent, slow)
  • × Mechanical ruling (limited complexity)
  • × Electron-beam direct write (too slow for production)

Codifiability

Explicit Knowledge 75%

Process recipes, exposure parameters, and equipment specifications are highly codified. However, optimization remains partially tacit.

Trainability

Transfer Ease 40%

Requires years of experience for process engineers. Equipment operation trainable in months, but troubleshooting and optimization require extensive tacit knowledge.

Automatability

Machine Execution 95%

Highly automated in production. Scanners run with minimal human intervention. AI-driven process control increasingly replacing human optimization.
